Chapter 12: European Colonialism in Africa (1880–1960)

The Scramble for Africa, formalized at the Berlin Conference of 1884–1885, saw European powers carve up the African continent with little regard for existing political boundaries, ethnic groups, or cultural identities. By 1914, nearly 90% of Africa was under European colonial control.

Key Developments:

1. The Conquest of West Africa
European powers used military force to subjugate West African kingdoms. The British conquest of the Ashanti Empire (1896) and the French campaigns in Dahomey (1892) involved systematic violence against civilian populations. Indigenous political structures were dismantled and replaced with colonial administrations that served European economic interests.

2. Forced Labor and Resource Extraction
Colonial regimes imposed forced labor systems to extract raw materials. In the Belgian Congo under Leopold II, rubber quotas were enforced through mutilation and hostage-taking, resulting in an estimated 10 million deaths between 1885 and 1908. The French corvée system in West Africa compelled unpaid labor on infrastructure projects that primarily benefited colonial commerce.

3. Land Dispossession
European settlers seized the most fertile agricultural land across southern and eastern Africa. In Kenya, the 1915 Crown Lands Ordinance declared all "unoccupied" land as Crown property, displacing the Kikuyu and Maasai peoples from their ancestral territories. In South Africa, the 1913 Natives Land Act restricted Black land ownership to just 7% of the country's territory.

4. Cultural Suppression
Colonial authorities systematically suppressed indigenous languages, religions, and educational traditions. Mission schools enforced European curricula while denigrating African knowledge systems. Traditional governance structures were either destroyed or co-opted into the colonial apparatus through indirect rule.

Economic Exploitation:

Colonial economies were structured to extract wealth from Africa for European benefit. Cash crop monocultures replaced subsistence farming, creating food insecurity. Export infrastructure — railways, ports, roads — was designed exclusively to move resources from the interior to coastal ports for shipment to Europe, not to connect African communities or foster local economic development.

Legacy:

The exploitation and subjugation of African peoples during the colonial period left lasting scars. Arbitrary borders drawn by European powers continue to fuel ethnic conflicts. Economic structures established during colonialism perpetuated dependency on raw material exports. The psychological impact of colonial racism and cultural suppression shaped post-independence identity struggles across the continent.
